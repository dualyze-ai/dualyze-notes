# How Alice Discovered Her Two Habit Notes Were 78% the Same

Alice has two notes about habits.

`Atomic Habits Notes.md` — written while reading James Clear's book.
`Habit Formation.md` — written two months later while researching psychology papers.

She forgot the first note existed when she wrote the second one.

Bob introduces Dualyze Notes.

---

## The problem: the same idea, written twice

---

**Alice:**
I found two notes that seem to cover the same ground. "Atomic Habits Notes" and "Habit Formation." I'm not sure which one to keep — or if I should merge them.

**Bob:**
Open `Atomic Habits Notes.md` and right-click → **Find similar notes**.

**Alice:**
The Similar Notes panel opened. It's showing:

```
Similar to: Atomic Habits Notes.md

78%  Merge candidate
Habit Formation.md
[Compare]  [Report]  [Create merge draft]
```

**Bob:**
78% is a strong overlap. That's in merge candidate territory.

**Alice:**
I wrote these two months apart and I thought they were different.

**Bob:**
They probably are different — but 78% of their vocabulary, headings, and links overlap. Let's look at exactly what.

---

## Step 1: Compare side by side

---

**Alice:**
I clicked **Compare**. Both notes are open side by side now.

Score breakdown at the top:

```
Overall: 78%

Headings: 85%   Keywords: 82%   Tags: 70%
Links: 60%      Title: 40%
```

Headings are 85% the same. That's why they felt interchangeable.

**Bob:**
Scroll to the keyword chips.

**Alice:**
**Common:** `habit`, `cue`, `routine`, `reward`, `identity`, `behavior`, `friction`, `environment design`, `habit loop`, `keystone habit`

**Unique to Atomic Habits Notes:** `James Clear`, `Four Laws`, `Make it obvious`, `Make it attractive`, `Make it easy`, `Make it satisfying`, `2-minute rule`, `habit stacking`

**Unique to Habit Formation:** `BJ Fogg`, `Tiny Habits`, `motivation wave`, `anchor habit`, `celebration`, `neuroplasticity`, `dopamine`, `basal ganglia`

**Bob:**
That's your answer. Both notes are about habits, but they're citing different authors — James Clear versus BJ Fogg — with different frameworks.

**Alice:**
I had no idea Habit Formation was based on BJ Fogg's work. I wrote it after reading some psychology articles and never connected it back to Atomic Habits.

**Bob:**
This is the most common pattern: same topic, different sources, written at different times. They feel like duplicates but they're actually two perspectives on the same subject.

> **💡 What does the score breakdown mean?**
> Dualyze Notes scores similarity across five dimensions. High heading overlap means the notes have the same structure. High keyword overlap means they cover the same concepts. The unique keyword chips show what each note contributes that the other doesn't — that's the most useful part.

## Step 2: Create the merge draft

---

**Alice:**
I clicked **Create merge draft**. 

In `Dualyze Merge Drafts/` there's a new file: `Atomic Habits Notes + Habit Formation — Merge Draft.md`

Here's what it looks like:

```markdown
# Atomic Habits Notes + Habit Formation — Merge Draft

_Generated by Dualyze Notes — review before using_

## Common Content

### The Habit Loop
cue → routine → reward...

### Identity-Based Change
habits as votes for the person you want to become...

### Environment Design
reducing friction, making good habits obvious...

## From Atomic Habits Notes only

### The Four Laws of Behavior Change
Make it obvious / attractive / easy / satisfying...

### 2-Minute Rule
scale any habit down to two minutes...

### Habit Stacking
attach new habits to existing ones...

## From Habit Formation only

### BJ Fogg's Tiny Habits Method
anchor habits, motivation wave, celebration...

### The Neuroscience
dopamine, basal ganglia, neuroplasticity...

---
Source: [[Atomic Habits Notes]], [[Habit Formation]]
```

**Alice:**
The originals are still untouched?

**Bob:**
Always. The merge draft is a scaffold for your editing. You decide what stays, what gets cut, what gets rewritten.

**Alice:**
This is exactly what I needed. I can see the Four Laws from Clear and the Tiny Habits method from Fogg side by side. They complement each other perfectly.

**Bob:**
You were about to delete one of them because they seemed redundant. Instead you found they cover different angles of the same topic.

**Alice:**
If I'd just read through both notes manually, I might have missed the BJ Fogg connection — it's buried in the middle of a long note.

**Bob:**
The unique content sections surface exactly what would be lost in a deletion. That's the point.

> **💡 What is a Merge Draft?**
> A Merge Draft is a scaffold document in `Dualyze Merge Drafts/`. It separates shared content from content unique to each note. The originals are never touched. The draft is a starting point — you decide what to keep, cut, or combine.
